Introduction

First Encounter

What is this brown rice? It's all sticky, muddy, and wet. It looks like a porridge, but was left to burn in the stove. It's hot, as steam rises from the bowl. What is this taste? it tastes bitter, but mildly comforting. What's with the smelly fish? Why is it in the bowl? Is this even food? Or is this all just wasted food?

Why does the saltiness of the fish, makes eating this more unique? It feels like a porridge, but somehow isn't? Maybe this is good on a rainy morning, watching the rain fall down as you eat idly on a window. The bitterness does feel good when you smell the earth as it got wet by the rain. But why does this work so well?

What the outsider sees as a confusing, possibly ruined porridge is, to a Filipino, a beloved comfort food: champorado at tuyo. The "brown rice" is glutinous rice or malagkit na kanin simmered with tablea, pure cacao tables introduced to the Philippines via the Manila-Acapulco galleon trade. The bitterness comes from the processed cacao. The "smelly fish" is tuyo, dried for preservation, used a lot in the country. Together, they create a contrast that elevates the this, something sweet at the same time salty, soft and mushy, but crisp as well. This dish is a generations-old ritual, tied to cold and rainy mornings, childhood, and the care of motherly figures.

Ethnographer's Reflection

Mula sa Kusina ng Alaala

The ritual of champorado and tuyo is a nostalgia of care and comfort. Growing up, I used to eat this during rainy days, wherein everything outside was so cold and uninviting. The warmth from the porridge, the bitter yet sweet taste, and the salty yet crunchy feeling of the tuyo provided a comforting sensation that was incomparable to anything else. It served as a means to reconnect to my childhood and to the loving care of my grandmother, who made this for me.

To this day, whenever I eat this, I am transported back to those rainy days where I feel nostalgic sensations which are difficult to describe. In addition, it is a memory that reminds me of care and love and therefore becomes very special for me.

Through this activity of writing about something common in Filipino life, I have realized that there is so much meaning behind something so simple. This is even the first time I pondered why tuyo works well with champorado.
